About Blood Donation

Today in the developed world, most blood donors are unpaid volunteers who donate blood for a community supply. In some countries, established supplies are limited and donors usually give blood when family or friends need a transfusion (directed donation).

Blood is a specialized bodily fluid that delivers necessary substances to the body's cells. It has four main components: plasma, red blood cells, white blood cells, and platelets. Blood has many different functions, including transporting oxygen and nutrients to the lungs and tissues.

Blood Types

There are four main blood types: A, B, AB, and O. It's important to know your blood type for safe transfusions. Blood type compatibility is crucial during emergency transfusions and surgeries. Additionally, some blood types are more common than others, which affects donation availability and demand.

Each blood type is also classified by the Rh factor, either positive (+) or negative (-). People with a negative Rh factor can donate to both positive and negative recipients, but can only receive negative blood, while positive Rh can receive both.

  • A: Has A antigens on the red cells and anti-B antibodies in the plasma.
  • B: Has B antigens on the red cells and anti-A antibodies in the plasma.
  • AB: Has both A and B antigens. Known as the universal recipient.
  • O: Has no A or B antigens. Known as the universal donor.
